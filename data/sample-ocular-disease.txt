Acute angle-closure glaucoma	Sudden painful red eye with mid-dilated fixed pupil, corneal edema, haloes around lights, nausea/vomiting, and markedly elevated IOP. Ocular emergency.
Hyphema	Blood in the anterior chamber, usually from blunt trauma. Risk of rebleeding and elevated IOP. Advise upright rest and shield.
Central retinal artery occlusion	Sudden painless monocular vision loss with a pale retina and cherry-red spot at the fovea. Ocular stroke equivalent.
Central retinal vein occlusion	Sudden painless vision loss with "blood and thunder" fundus: diffuse retinal hemorrhages, dilated tortuous veins, and disc edema.
Papilledema	Bilateral optic disc swelling due to raised intracranial pressure. Look for blurred disc margins and loss of spontaneous venous pulsation.
Anterior uveitis	Painful red eye with photophobia, ciliary flush, cells and flare in the anterior chamber, and possible hypopyon. Often associated with HLA-B27.
Bacterial keratitis	Painful red eye with a corneal infiltrate/ulcer, often contact-lens related. A culture-worthy sight-threatening infection.
Herpes simplex keratitis	Dendritic corneal ulcer that stains with fluorescein. Reduced corneal sensation. Avoid steroids alone.
Diabetic retinopathy	Microaneurysms, dot-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates, and in proliferative disease neovascularization. Leading cause of vision loss in working-age adults.
Age-related macular degeneration	Central vision loss in older adults. Dry form shows drusen; wet form shows choroidal neovascularization and fluid/hemorrhage.
Retinal detachment	Flashes, floaters, and a curtain/shadow over the visual field. A tear allows fluid under the neurosensory retina.
Open-angle glaucoma	Slowly progressive optic neuropathy with increased cup-to-disc ratio and arcuate visual field defects, usually with elevated IOP and open angles.
Amblyopia	Reduced best-corrected acuity from abnormal visual development, due to strabismus, anisometropia, or deprivation. Treat early.
Horner syndrome	Ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis from a disrupted oculosympathetic pathway. Anisocoria greater in the dark.
Third nerve palsy	"Down and out" eye with ptosis. A painful pupil-involving palsy suggests a compressive aneurysm until proven otherwise.
